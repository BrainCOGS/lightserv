Injection site recovery

One of the main applications of the Core Facility is to identify the location and spread of an injection. In this example, Dr. Jess Verpeut injected DREADDs with an mcherry tag into bilateral Crus I of the cerebellum to study the effects of perturbation on behavior. A component of this analysis is measuring the volume and spread of the injection site throughout the cerebellum. The figure above shows our injection site localization for a single brain in the cohort. After imaging, brains were aligned to the Princeton Mouse Atlas, a light-sheet analog to the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas. A max projection of the atlas-aligned injection channel (647 nm) is shown in coronal and sagittal views in the left panels above. We then used a basic filter + threshold + connected components approach to create a 3D mask of the bulk injection site. Max projections of this mask in both views are shown in the middle panels above. The right panel shows the masks overlaid on top of a medial slice of the Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas for reference. We then identified which brain regions contained the injection and the volume of injection in each by determining the number of voxels of overlap between the 3D segmentation mask and the 3D atlas:

The code for creating the mask, making the figure and making the dataframe of injection volumes can be found in this Jupyter notebook.

Technical specs:
Species: Mouse
Clearing protocol: iDISCO+ (with immunostaining)
Primary antibody: Rabbit antiRFP
Secondary antibody: Donkey antiRabbit 647
Microscope: LaVision
Imaging resolution/pixel size: 1.1x (5.91 microns)
Imaging channels: Registration (488 nm; not shown), injection site recovery (647 nm; shown above)
Visualization: Python/matplotlib

Automatic registration to the Franklin-Paxinos Mouse Brain Atlas

In November 2020, we added the three dimensional atlas developed by Chon et al. 2019 (PI Yongsoo Kim; Penn State; https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-019-13057-w) as an option to which we can register your mouse brain light sheet images using our automatic brain registration pipeline, BrainPipe. This atlas merges the widely adopted Franklin-Paxinos (FP) labels into the Allen Institute's Common Coordinate Framework (CCF). By aligning your data to this atlas, you can overlay the FP region boundaries on your light sheet data as shown above. This atlas is only suitable for coronal slice viewing (the resolution is poor in the horizontal and sagittal views).

Showing the boundaries of brain regions like this is possible for the other atlases. To learn more about how to access the different atlases and their boundaries in Neuroglancer, see this tutorial: https://brainmaps.princeton.edu/2020/12/displaying-atlas-region-boundaries-in-neuroglancer/

Technical specs:
Species: Mouse
Clearing protocol: iDISCO abbreviated clearing
Microscope: LaVision
Imaging resolution/pixel size: 1.1x (5.91 microns)
Imaging channels: Registration (488 nm; shown above), injection site detection (555 nm; not shown)
Visualization: neuroglancer

Transsynaptic viral tracing

The original use case of the Core Facility. Pisano et al. (in prep.) used light sheet imaging to map anatomical pathways between the cerebellum and sensorimotor, associative, and modulatory regions of the forebrain. Shown above is a three dimensional visualization of cell centers (light green) that were traced using the anterograde transsynaptic tracer herpes simplex virus type 1 (H129) and the retrograde tracer pseudorabies virus (Bartha). Also shown are cerebellar subregions from the Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas (PMA), a modified version of the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (AMBA). The PMA extends the AMBA to include the posterior cerebellum, and it improves registration accuracy for light sheet images.

Technical specs:
Species: Mouse
Clearing protocol: iDISCO+ (with immunostaining)
Microscope: LaVision
Imaging resolution/pixel size: 4x (1.63 microns)
Imaging channels: Registration (488 nm), Cell detection (647 nm)
Cell detection method: https://github.com/PrincetonUniversity/3dunet
Visualization: neuroglancer

c-Fos thalamic expression

Verpeut et al. (in prep.) used light sheet imaging to trace brain-wide c-Fos expression after a Y-maze reversal task. Shown above is an animation of a sagittal sweep through the brain of a mouse subject that underwent the task. The left panel shows the 2D sagittal slices and the right panel shows the 3D context of these slices. In both panels, the raw data and selected thalamic regions of the Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas are shown. At the midpoint of the sweep, cell center labels (yellow) obtained from the Clearmap cell detection algorithm are turned on and the animation sweeps in reverse. The cell centers shown are a subsample of the total cell centers detected, but are representative of the spatial distribution.

We provide analysis tools for cell counting/pooling in the Allen and Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas ontologies: https://github.com/PrincetonUniversity/lightsheet_helper_scripts/blob/master/ontology_analysis/

Technical specs:
Species: Mouse
Clearing protocol: iDISCO+ (with immunostaining)
Microscope: LaVision
Imaging resolution/pixel size: 1.3x (5 microns)
Imaging channels: Registration (488 nm; not shown), Cell detection (647 nm; shown above)
Cell detection method: https://github.com/PrincetonUniversity/ClearMapCluster
Visualization: neuroglancer

Neuronexus probe detection

Oostland et al. used light sheet imaging to do a post-experiment verification of the precise anatomical regions of a Neuronexus probe implanted in the lateral cerebellum. The probe has four shanks which were coated in CM-Dil to increase their signal brightness. The top left panel shows a coronal slice of the brain with the image (greyscale) and selected regions of the Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas (colored overlay). The black vertical strips in the image are the CM-Dil signal, marking the location of the probe in this section. The yellow circles are annotations added in Neuroglancer to mark the locations along each of the four shanks in multiple sections. The top right panel shows the 3D view of the annotations in the context of the brain regions.

The coordinates and brain region of each annotation can be exported to a CSV file from Neuroglancer for further analysis. The bottom panel shows the CSV file that was exported from this example. Each row represents a different annotation. The columns that are blank here are there to support other types of annotations, such as lines and ellipsoids which are not used in this example. The coordinates are in the registered atlas space.

Technical specs:
Species: Mouse
Clearing protocol: iDISCO abbreviated clearing
Microscope: LaVision
Imaging resolution/pixel size: 1.3x (5 microns)
Imaging channels: Registration (488 nm; not shown), Probe detection (555 nm; shown above)
Visualization: neuroglancer

Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas

The Princeton Mouse Brain Atlas (PMA) was created to achieve greater registration performance than the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (AMBA) for light sheet data. (a) Sagittal views demonstrate differences between the AMBA, left and the PMA, right. The red dotted box indicates the caudal limit of the AMBA. To map between PMA and ABA space, ABA annotations were transformed into PMA space. (b) Registration of whole-brain light-sheet volumes to the PMA. Light-sheet volume of an individual brain (green) overlaid with PMA (red) at different stages of registration. Median discrepancy is shown for each stage of alignment. (c) PMA cerebellar annotations. The red dotted box indicates newly annotated areas. (d) PMA cerebellar hierarchy depicting structure ontology and shows relative substructure size contributions. Abbreviations: PM, paramedian lobule; PF, paraflocculi; CP, copula pyramidis; Fl, flocculus.
